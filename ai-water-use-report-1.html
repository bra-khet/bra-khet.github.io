§ 01

Executive Summary

The rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence (AI) infrastructure has fundamentally altered the resource consumption profile of the global digital economy. As hyperscale data centers expand to accommodate the high-density computing clusters required for generative AI models, the thermodynamic necessity of thermal rejection has placed unprecedented strain on regional water resources. This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level analysis of direct and indirect data center water consumption in the United States as of 2026, with specific focus on highly stressed, over-allocated watersheds.[1]

A central objective of this investigation is the systematic verification of claims presented in the Florida Water Pollution Control Operators Association (FWPCOA) article, "Myths vs. Reality: Data Centers And Water Usage," published January 23, 2026.[1] Verification against 2024–2026 data from the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL), the International Energy Agency (IEA), the Houston Advanced Research Center (HARC), and hyperscaler sustainability disclosures reveals that several volumetric baselines cited in the FWPCOA article require critical revision to reflect the exponential escalation driven by AI hardware density.[3]

§ 02

Theoretical Foundation: The Thermodynamics of Data Center Cooling

Data-center thermal rejection is immutably governed by the second law of thermodynamics: electrical energy consumed by CPUs and GPUs is entirely converted into heat, which must be continuously transferred to an external environmental sink. The efficiency and resource intensity of this process depend entirely on the physical mechanism of heat transfer.

Open-Loop Evaporative Cooling

Open-loop evaporative cooling has historically been the preferred methodology due to its superior energy efficiency. This system exploits the latent heat of vaporization — approximately 2,260 kilojoules per kilogram of water — by forcing ambient air across wetted media or spraying water into cooling towers.[8] However, approximately 70–80% of water withdrawn is permanently evaporated, leaving behind concentrated dissolved solids (wastewater "blowdown").[1]

Closed-Loop Systems

Closed-loop cooling (dry coolers, direct-to-chip liquid, dielectric immersion) relies on sensible heat transfer. Because air's specific heat capacity is low (~1.006 kJ/kg-K), this requires substantially higher volumetric flow rates, large energy-intensive fans, and mechanical chillers.[6] Liquid cooling captures heat efficiently at the silicon level, minimizing direct water evaporation on-site — but requires more electricity for secondary heat rejection, driving up Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE).

WUE
Water Usage Effectiveness = liters consumed per kWh of IT equipment energy. The legacy industry average for open-loop evaporative systems is ~1.8 L/kWh. Highly optimized hybrid or closed-loop architectures can achieve below 0.3 L/kWh.[17] But evaluating WUE in isolation ignores the indirect water footprint: the LBNL calculates that while direct U.S. data center water consumption was 66 billion liters in 2023, the indirect footprint via the power grid was approximately 800 billion liters — more than 12× greater.[3]
The core tradeoff: Rack power densities have increased dramatically — from 5–15 kW for conventional cloud racks to 50–132 kW for AI-optimized NVIDIA Blackwell racks, with next-generation Rubin Ultra NVL576 configurations projected at up to 600 kW–1 MW per rack by 2027–2028.[10] This density makes traditional air cooling thermodynamically impossible, forcing rapid adoption of liquid cooling — but at the cost of higher electrical parasitic loads and expanded indirect water footprints.

§ 03

Practical Applications: Mitigation Trajectories

Dominant hyperscalers have initiated aggressive hydrological mitigation strategies in 2025–2026, centering on reclaimed wastewater and zero-evaporation reference architectures.

Amazon Web Services (AWS)

AWS is aggressively pursuing its 2030 "water positive" pledge by integrating recycled wastewater at over 120 locations globally. By 2024, AWS reported reaching 53% of its water-positive goal, operating at a global average WUE of 0.15 L/kWh. AWS projects that combining recycled water with optimized evaporative cooling can reduce potable water reliance by up to 85% compared to conventional methodologies.[20]

Microsoft

In late 2024, Microsoft launched a new datacenter design specifically optimized for AI workloads that consumes zero water for cooling. This architecture uses chip-level closed-loop liquid cooling networks; once filled during construction, the primary coolant circulates indefinitely without evaporation. Microsoft estimates this reference design saves 125 million liters per facility annually. Pilot deployments are active in Phoenix, Arizona, and Mt. Pleasant, Wisconsin — though the tradeoff is a higher electrical burden.[18]

Google

Google uses a localized "climate-conscious cooling" framework: evaporative cooling in water-abundant regions; air-cooled chillers or non-potable water in arid regions. In 2023, Google consumed 6.1 billion gallons of potable water globally. In 2024, the company replenished 4.5 billion gallons (64% of freshwater consumption) through agricultural efficiency, leak repairs, and Colorado River Basin ecosystem restoration.[25]

§ 04

Verification of FWPCOA Article Claims

The FWPCOA published "Myths vs. Reality: Data Centers And Water Usage" in early 2026.[1] A rigorous verification of its five primary claims against 2024–2026 empirical data yields the following assessments:

Claim 1: 75–90% of data centers worldwide rely on water-based cooling; only 10–25% are completely water-free. Large facilities predominantly use open-loop evaporative systems. ✓ Accurate

The structural legacy of the data center industry is built upon the energy efficiency of evaporative cooling towers. LBNL and industry analyses confirm that completely water-free environments are largely restricted to smaller enterprise facilities, specialized edge deployments, or specific hyperscale builds in highly restricted desert environments.[1]

Claim 2: Closed-loop systems reduce freshwater use by up to 70% and return ~90–95% as wastewater. ⚠ Needs Revision

While accurate that hybrid liquid-air systems can reduce evaporative consumption by ~70% vs. legacy open-loop, the "90–95% wastewater return" assertion is thermodynamically flawed for a true closed-loop design. A genuine closed-loop system requires only an initial charge of coolant with virtually zero ongoing withdrawal — it does not continuously withdraw massive water volumes to return 95% of them. The FWPCOA conflates the low consumptive ratio of certain hybrid systems with the operational mechanics of true closed-loop liquid cooling.[1]

Claim 3: A medium facility uses ~100 million gallons annually; hyperscale peak usage reaches 1–5 million gallons per day. ✓ Generally Accurate

The 100 million gallon annual figure for a "medium" facility is representative of older enterprise infrastructure. The hyperscale peak metric of 1–5 million gallons per day is thoroughly corroborated by 2025–2026 data. Facilities engaged in high-density AI training can demand upwards of 3.7–5 million gallons daily during peak summer heat when evaporative systems run at maximum capacity.[28]

Claim 4: U.S. total consumption is 449 million gallons per day (2021 baseline), with 70–80% evaporation and ~1.8 L/kWh legacy WUE. ✗ Severely Outdated

The total volumetric claim is severely outdated. The 2024 LBNL U.S. Data Center Energy Usage Report confirmed direct water consumption of 66 billion liters (17.4 billion gallons annually, ~47.7 million gallons per day) in 2023, while indirect consumption at thermoelectric plants reached 800 billion liters (~578 million gallons per day).[3] The combined 2023 footprint heavily eclipses the cited 2021 data. Projections suggest consumption will double or quadruple by 2028. The evaporation fraction (70–80%) and legacy WUE (1.8 L/kWh) remain thermodynamically accurate for older open-loop infrastructure.

Claim 5: Google and AWS are examples of operators utilizing reclaimed water. ✓ Highly Accurate

Hyperscaler sustainability disclosures from 2025–2026 confirm aggressive scaling of reclaimed wastewater infrastructure. AWS is actively targeting 120 locations for recycled water integration.[20] Google extensively utilizes non-potable canal and industrial water at massive campuses in Belgium and the Netherlands.[24] This trend is expanding rapidly.

§ 05

National Data & Trend Analysis (2026 U.S. Focus)

The integration of AI has decoupled data center resource trajectories from historical norms. Prior to 2020, massive shifts toward cloud virtualization allowed computing output to grow while electricity and water usage remained relatively flat.[32] Generative AI — relying on thousands of synchronized chips — has shattered this efficiency plateau.

According to the 2024 LBNL report, total direct water consumption by U.S. data centers in 2023 stood at 66 billion liters. Hyperscale and colocation facilities accounted for an overwhelming 84% of this total.[3] Direct water consumption is projected to double or quadruple by 2028; under high-growth scenarios, hyperscale facilities alone are forecasted to consume 60–124 billion liters annually by decade's end. The indirect water footprint at thermoelectric plants dwarfs direct consumption — approximately 800 billion liters in 2023, projected to exceed 1,500 billion liters by 2028.

The AI Power Multiplier: A standard internet search consumes ~0.3 Wh. An advanced generative AI query requires 2.9–40 Wh depending on model complexity.[35] In 2023, data centers consumed 176–183 TWh (4.0–4.4% of U.S. grid demand). By 2028, the IEA and LBNL project data centers could consume 6.7–12.0% of total U.S. electricity.[34]

§ 06

U.S. Location Deep-Dives: Priority High-Risk Areas

The intersection of unprecedented AI infrastructure expansion and geographic water scarcity represents the most critical environmental conflict in the technology sector. Data center development is heavily clustered in specific corridors due to fiber-optic latency, tax incentives, and energy pricing — regardless of underlying hydrological carrying capacity.

Northern Virginia — Loudoun, Fairfax, and Prince William counties — is universally recognized as "Data Center Alley." It is the most concentrated data center market globally, handling an estimated 70% of worldwide internet traffic across 290+ distinct buildings with aggregate power demand exceeding 5,400 MW.[38] [11]

In 2023, Loudoun County alone directly consumed approximately 900 million gallons of water, pushing the broader Northern Virginia footprint to nearly 2 billion gallons — a 63% increase from 2019 levels. While data centers account for only 1% of total volumetric Washington Metropolitan Area (WMA) withdrawals, their high evaporative loss means they represent 9% of annual consumptive use, rising to 12% during peak summer.[11] [39]

ICPRB baseline models project data center water use in the WMA to average 22 MGD by 2050, with peak usage exceeding 80 MGD. The ICPRB has issued stark warnings that under extreme drought conditions, this unchecked consumptive use could trigger mandatory municipal water restrictions across D.C., Maryland, and Virginia by 2030.[40] Facilities in Loudoun Water's jurisdiction already utilize over 700 million gallons of reclaimed water annually via a dedicated 20-mile "purple pipe" network.[39]

The Phoenix metropolitan area has become a primary hyperscale destination despite severe, ongoing hydrological deficits and the over-allocation of the Colorado River system.

According to the 2024 Ceres report "Drained by Data," data centers in the Phoenix region consumed approximately 385 million gallons of water directly for cooling, while indirectly consuming 2.9 billion gallons through electricity generation.[12] Peak WUE metrics exceeding 9 L/kWh have been recorded during summer heat waves, as hybrid cooling systems shift entirely to evaporative mode to prevent hardware thermal throttling.[44]

Ceres projects that regional data center cooling water demand could spike by 870% to over 3.7 billion gallons annually, severely straining a water table already operating under federal Tier 1 shortage declarations. Because the Colorado River basin is a strict zero-sum equation, this growth will invariably force municipalities to acquire agricultural water rights, accelerating the retirement of arable land.[45] [12]

Texas is experiencing an unprecedented AI infrastructure build-out, highlighted by the planned 1.2 GW "Stargate One" facility spanning 940 acres. As of late 2025, Texas hosted over 400 data centers.

The Houston Advanced Research Center (HARC) estimates existing facilities consumed approximately 25 billion gallons of water (direct + indirect) in 2025, accounting for 0.4% of total state use. ERCOT load forecasts project Texas data center water consumption will reach 29–161 billion gallons annually by 2030 — up to 2.7% of total state water supply.[4] [47]

HARC emphasizes that this massive industrial growth is occurring outside the current Texas State Water Plan, creating a systemic planning blind spot for regional authorities already grappling with a 4.8 million acre-foot structural deficit. Deep groundwater aquifers (Ogallala, Trinity) in targeted rural areas like Abilene lack rapid recharge rates, making reliance on them for constant baseload industrial cooling inherently unsustainable.[47]

Iowa (Council Bluffs): Google's Council Bluffs campus relies heavily on local groundwater and the Missouri River aquifer. In 2024, the site consumed 1 billion gallons of potable water, functioning as Google's most water-intensive location globally and drawing criticism from local geological surveys.[54]

Oregon (The Dalles): Google facilities consume approximately one-third of the city's total water supply — over 300 million gallons annually — driving conflicts over the city's pursuit of additional groundwater rights in the Mount Hood National Forest.[56]

Georgia (Atlanta): Metro Atlanta hosts over 50 facilities with 40 more proposed. The region relies almost entirely on small surface watersheds — geographic granite bedrock severely limits groundwater access. Conservationists warn unchecked evaporative cooling will limit critical return flows to the Chattahoochee River system.[49]

California: Data centers consumed an estimated 50 billion liters of water in 2023, projected to reach 116 billion liters by 2028.[51] A UC Riverside study warns that meeting the peak summer water demands of AI data centers will require billions of dollars in new municipal water infrastructure.[52]

Nevada: High desert environments force operators to rely heavily on dry cooling, though hybrid systems still extract millions of gallons locally, sparking community pushback. The Desert Research Institute (DRI) has published detailed regional impact assessments.[53]

§ 08

Mitigation, Innovation & the 2026 Policy Landscape

In 2025–2026, U.S. legislative posture transitioned from passive observation to active intervention. Three distinct policy trajectories have emerged:

Reporting Mandates

Transparency First

California AB 1577, SB 57; Iowa HF 2447; Michigan SB 762 — mandate data center operators report PUE, WUE, and total volumetric water consumption to state energy commissions. Goal: lift the veil of NDAs shielding operations from public scrutiny.[13]

Engineering Mandates

Closed-Loop Requirements

South Carolina S.902 / H.4583 "Data Center Responsibility Act" and Kansas SB 400 would legally mandate closed-loop liquid cooling systems — zero net water withdrawal, zero wastewater discharge. Explicitly prohibit municipal water/groundwater extraction for cooling.[13] [14]

Incentive Programs

Reclaimed Water Credits

Virginia SB 30 would condition lucrative data center tax credits on the utilization of reclaimed wastewater or advanced environmental management practices — creating financial incentives aligned with ecological priorities.[13]

The Economic-Environmental Tradeoff: The transition to closed-loop or air-cooled chillers completely eliminates potable water evaporation but imposes a massive energy penalty. Operating chillers in high-ambient environments like Phoenix or Austin requires more electricity — often generated by fossil fuels. This increases Scope 2 carbon emissions and expands the indirect water footprint at the power plant level. Regulators and hyperscalers must navigate this delicate balance: preserving local water security at the potential expense of regional grid stability and global carbon decarbonization goals.

§ 09

Balanced Debate: Siting AI Infrastructure in Stressed Basins

Pro-Expansion Perspective

  • Data centers represent less than 1% of national water withdrawals — a fraction of agriculture or thermoelectric generation.[3]
  • Hyperscaler WUE metrics have dropped 30–50% over five years. AWS operates at 0.15 L/kWh globally; Microsoft at 0.27 L/kWh.[31]
  • Aggressive transition to recycled wastewater and zero-evaporation designs effectively decouples growth from municipal drinking supplies.[18]
  • Massive capital investments in data centers catalyze grid modernization, accelerating retirement of water-intensive coal plants in favor of wind, solar, and nuclear — which possess virtually zero indirect water footprints.[27]

Critical Perspective

  • Macro-level percentages obscure devastating local impacts. Geographic clustering concentrates immense peak demands in specific vulnerable watersheds.[76]
  • Tech companies frequently use NDAs to hide resource consumption from the public, preventing accurate municipal planning.[77]
  • Corporate "water positive" replenishment pledges — funding distant agricultural leak repairs — do not offset the immediate physical loss of millions of gallons from local municipal pipes.[79]
  • Staggering energy demands are keeping legacy fossil-fuel power plants online longer than anticipated, worsening the indirect water footprint and delaying climate goals.[76]

§ 10

Conclusion

The expansion of AI infrastructure represents a profound paradigm shift in industrial resource consumption. The thermodynamic realities of high-density computing dictate that heat rejection will command vast quantities of either water or electricity — there is no costless option. The verification of FWPCOA claims and the synthesis of 2024–2026 data from LBNL, HARC, and the IEA confirm this unavoidable energy-water tradeoff at every level of analysis.

To ensure sustainable deployment in water-constrained regions, policymakers and industry leaders must adopt a holistic, systemic approach:

First, state and federal regulators must standardize and mandate reporting of site-level PUE and WUE metrics alongside indirect energy-water calculations. You cannot manage a resource hidden behind corporate NDAs.

Second, municipalities should condition zoning approvals and tax incentives on the utilization of non-potable, reclaimed wastewater for cooling.

Third, rather than instituting blanket mandates, policies should reflect local realities. In hydrologically stressed basins like Arizona and Texas, zero-evaporation liquid cooling should be required despite the energy penalty. In regions with constrained electrical grids but abundant water, highly efficient evaporative cooling using reclaimed water may remain the optimal systemic choice.

Ultimately, future infrastructure planning must view data centers not as isolated buildings, but as massive, interdependent nodes within the broader water-energy nexus.

Glossary

WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness)

Liters of water consumed per kilowatt-hour of IT equipment energy. The primary metric for evaluating direct on-site water efficiency. Lower is better. Does not capture indirect grid water footprint.

PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)

Total facility energy divided by IT equipment energy. A PUE of 1.0 means all power goes to computing; higher values indicate overhead from cooling and other systems.

Open-Loop Evaporative Cooling

Cooling system that continuously withdraws fresh water, evaporating 70–80% to reject heat. Highly energy-efficient but severely consumptive of local water resources.

Closed-Loop Liquid Cooling

Cooling architecture using a sealed, recirculating liquid circuit (water, dielectric fluid) with no net water evaporation. Higher electrical cost; may increase indirect water footprint.

Indirect Water Footprint

Water consumed by thermoelectric power plants supplying a facility's electricity. Can be 4–12× greater than direct on-site evaporation, depending on regional grid mix.

Blowdown

Wastewater discharged from evaporative cooling towers to remove concentrated dissolved solids. Represents ~20–30% of water withdrawal in open-loop systems.

Hyperscale Facility

A data center operating at massive scale (typically 100 MW+ capacity), owned by large cloud providers (AWS, Google, Microsoft, Meta). Dominates industry water and energy consumption at 84% of the U.S. total.

LBNL

Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory — a U.S. Department of Energy research facility that publishes the definitive U.S. Data Center Energy Usage Reports, the primary empirical baseline for this analysis.
