Preliminary Statement

This Appeal Is Founded Entirely on Your Own Policy

!

The suppression of Happily Ever After is not a close call. Under the written text of GlobalComix's own March 24, 2026 AI content policy, the workflow used to produce this comic is explicitly categorized as acceptable. The enforcement action applied to it is a direct product of algorithmic failure — not policy compliance.

This appeal does not ask GlobalComix to make an exception. It does not ask the platform to change its standards, accommodate a gray area, or extend special treatment. It asks GlobalComix to apply the policy it already wrote — accurately, transparently, and consistently — to a creator who has operated in good faith under those terms.

The following four grounds for appeal are each anchored to specific, cited language from GlobalComix's own published policy documentation. The creator's documented workflow satisfies every stated criterion for acceptable AI-assisted content. The shadow ban is the error. Reinstatement is the correction.

The Case

Four Grounds for Immediate Reinstatement

  • Ground One
    The Workflow of Happily Ever After Is Explicitly Permitted Under the March 24, 2026 Policy

    GlobalComix's updated AI content policy draws a clear, written distinction between prohibited and acceptable AI use. The prohibition targets works where visual components are created "entirely via generative AI text-to-image prompts, lacking a broader human-led creative workflow."

    The policy separately and explicitly names the category of work that is permitted:

    GlobalComix AI Content Policy — March 24, 2026 (Acceptable AI Use) "…the use of custom trained visual generation models, provided they are trained exclusively on original, owned artwork and require extensive human-led character design, world-building, and final compositional assembly."

    The production methodology of Happily Ever After satisfies each element of this definition:

    "Characters are trained using custom LoRA models built from curated datasets. Scenes are generated iteratively, often dozens of times, with adjustments to pose, lighting, expression, and consistency. Images are selected, rejected, refined, and aligned to match narrative intent."

    • Writing and revising scripts.
    • Designing character arcs and long-term continuity.
    • Training custom character models for consistency.
    • Iterative scene generation and selection.
    • Panel composition.

    "AI assists with rendering, but it does not create the story, characters, structure, or meaning. Those elements come from deliberate human authorship."

    — u/DoctorZacharySmith, public workflow documentation

    Custom LoRAs trained on original character datasets: policy-compliant. Human-led character design: policy-compliant. Iterative compositional assembly: policy-compliant. The content was shadow-banned despite satisfying the exact criteria the policy establishes for acceptable work.

    ▲

    The suppression action was applied to content that, under GlobalComix's own written policy, is permitted. The action is inconsistent with the policy it claims to enforce.

  • Ground Two
    The Automated Enforcement Mechanism Is Technically Incapable of Evaluating This Workflow

    GlobalComix's March 2026 enforcement wave was executed primarily through automated algorithmic detection — tools designed to identify the visual characteristics of generative output. These systems operate on the final rendered image. They do not have access to, and cannot evaluate, the production process that generated it.

    A LoRA-trained workflow and a prompt-only workflow can produce visually similar outputs. The distinction between them — the iterative selection, the character training, the scripted narrative direction — is invisible to image-scanning algorithms. The platform's own policy acknowledges this: it places the burden of demonstrating human authorship on the creator, precisely because automated detection alone cannot make this determination.

    The suppression of Happily Ever After is therefore a false positive — a product of the known limitations of automated content moderation, not a finding of policy violation. Using an instrument that cannot measure human labor to adjudicate the sufficiency of human labor is not enforcement. It is error.

    GlobalComix AI Content Policy — March 24, 2026 (Creator Obligations) Platform policy places the ultimate "burden of proof" on the uploader to demonstrate compliance — an acknowledgment that automated detection is insufficient for this determination and that human review is required.
    ▲

    A suppression action taken by a detection system that is structurally incapable of evaluating the relevant evidence is not a valid enforcement action. It requires human review and correction.

  • Ground Three
    The Suppression Was Executed Without Notice — Denying the Creator Any Opportunity to Respond

    Shadow banning, by design, does not notify the affected creator. Happily Ever After was removed from GlobalComix's search index and all discovery algorithms without any communication to the creator: no notice of the action, no identification of the alleged violation, no opportunity to submit compliance documentation, and no defined appeal pathway.

    GlobalComix's own policy explicitly contemplates a process by which creators can demonstrate the human-authorship of their work. The policy establishes the creator's right to submit documentation. That process is rendered meaningless if creators are not told that they are required to use it.

    The creator discovered the suppression independently — by observing that the series had disappeared from search results — and has since produced a detailed, documented account of the production workflow. That documentation has never been formally evaluated. It exists. It is available. It has not been reviewed.

    EU Digital Services Act — Transparency Standard (Applicable Regulatory Context) Platform governance frameworks in major jurisdictions increasingly require that visibility-reduction actions be disclosed to the affected user with sufficient information to enable a meaningful response. Covert suppression without notification contravenes emerging global standards for platform transparency.
    ▲

    Suppressing content without notification — and without permitting the creator to submit the compliance documentation the platform's own policy anticipates — is procedurally indefensible. The policy's burden-of-proof mechanism cannot function without disclosure that it has been triggered.

  • Ground Four
    The Platform Applied Stricter Standards to This Creator Than to Its Own Commercial AI Operations

    During the same enforcement window in which Happily Ever After was shadow-banned, GlobalComix was actively deploying AI-powered localization tools obtained through its acquisition of INKR. These tools — which automate the translation and lettering of comics at scale — are generative AI applied to creative content distribution. They operate without the iterative human oversight, character training, or narrative direction documented in the Happily Ever After workflow.

    The March 24 policy as applied produces the following outcome: a creator using a custom-trained LoRA with extensive human editorial direction, scripted narrative, and original character design is shadow-banned with no notice. The platform's own backend AI localization pipeline — producing automated, algorithmically generated text for creator content — is endorsed, promoted, and monetized.

    This is not a judgment on the legitimacy of AI localization. It is an observation that the enforcement standard applied to u/DoctorZacharySmith does not reflect the standard the platform applies to its own operations. A governance framework that prohibits in creators what it practices institutionally is not enforcing a policy. It is enforcing a preference.

    ▲

    Selective enforcement — penalizing creator AI use while commercially deploying AI through its own platform infrastructure — undermines the neutrality and legitimacy of the moderation action applied to this work.

The Ask

Relief Requested

The creator respectfully requests the following specific remedies from GlobalComix administration. These requests are proportionate, reasonable, and each directly addresses a discrete harm caused by the enforcement action.

  1. Immediate Reinstatement of Full Search Visibility and Discovery Indexing

    Restore Happily Ever After to GlobalComix's search index, recommendation feeds, and all discovery algorithms from which it was removed. The series should appear in searches and recommendations exactly as it did prior to March 24, 2026. This is the primary and most urgent relief.

  2. Restoration of Full Monetization Eligibility

    Reinstate eligibility for GlobalComix Gold subscription revenue and PDF sales. Review and remediate any revenue withheld during the period of suppression. The platform's own policy explicitly grants monetization eligibility to AI-assisted content demonstrating "sufficient original artistry" — a standard this workflow demonstrably meets.

  3. Written Acknowledgment of the Moderation Action and Its Basis

    Provide a formal, written communication confirming that a visibility-reduction action was applied to this content, identifying the specific policy provision under which the action was taken, and confirming the basis on which reinstatement is granted. The platform's current practice of non-acknowledgment is itself a harm — it prevents the creator from understanding or responding to the enforcement action.

  4. Human Review of Submitted Workflow Documentation

    Conduct a formal review of the creator's documented production methodology — scripts, LoRA training records, iterative generation logs, and character design archives — by a qualified human moderator. Automated detection is structurally insufficient to evaluate this workflow. The policy's own compliance mechanism requires human review. That review has not yet occurred.

  5. A Defined, Accessible Appeal Pathway for Future Enforcement Actions

    Establish and communicate a clear process by which creators subject to shadow banning or soft delisting can: (a) receive formal notification of the action, (b) understand the specific policy basis, and (c) submit compliance documentation for human review. The absence of this pathway is not a minor procedural gap — it is the mechanism by which the current harm was perpetuated.
