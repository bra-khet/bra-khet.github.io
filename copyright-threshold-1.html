Did I Do Enough?

Illustrating the Threshold for Sufficient Creative Input in AI-Generated Content Under U.S. Copyright Law.

U.S. copyright law requires human authorship. Machines operating without creative human intervention do not qualify. This interactive report breaks down the U.S. Copyright Office's 2025 guidelines to demonstrate exactly where the line is drawn.

1. The Legal Landscape & Precedents

This section visualizes the current legal standing based on recent judicial decisions (*Thaler v. Perlmutter*, 2023-2026) and Copyright Office guidance. The core principle is that copyright protects human control over expressive elements. Prompts alone act as a "black box" and do not confer control.

❌ Insufficient (Uncopyrightable)

  • Purely AI-generated material (Thaler/DABUS).
  • Outputs generated solely via text prompts, regardless of prompt length or detail.
  • Minor, trivial (*de minimis*) edits to an AI output.

✔ Sufficient (Copyrightable)

  • Creative modifications, additions, or deletions of AI output.
  • Perceptible human expressive inputs integrated into the output (*Rose Enigma*).
  • Creative selection and arrangement of multiple AI outputs (*Zarya of the Dawn*).

Appendix: Key Legal Terminology

Originality (*Feist* test)

The constitutional requirement that a work be created independently (not copied) and possess at least a "modicum of creativity." "Sweat of the brow" (pure effort) is insufficient.

Human Authorship

The requirement that a human being must control and determine the final expressive elements of a work. Non-humans (animals, machines, divine beings) cannot be authors.

Expressive Elements

The actual words, shapes, colors, or arrangement perceived in the final work. Ideas, facts, and underlying algorithms are not protected, only their specific *expression*.

De minimis

A legal concept meaning "too small or trivial to count." For example, arranging only two AI images, or changing three words in a 500-word generated essay, falls below the threshold of registrable creativity.

The "Black Box"

The USCO's characterization of generative AI. Because the user inputs a prompt but the AI determines the exact visual or textual output unpredictably, the user lacks sufficient control over the final expression.
