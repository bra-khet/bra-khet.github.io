USCO 2025 — The Bar Is Low by Design

Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service, 499 U.S. 340, 345 (1991), held that copyright requires only "a modicum of creativity" — and that this standard is "extremely low." The USCO's 2025 AI Policy Report applies the same threshold to human–AI works: even selecting which AI output to use, or adding a single edit, can constitute sufficient human authorship. The Office has explicitly registered works where the human contribution was limited to selection and minimal arrangement.

Legal Foundation — USCO 2025 & Feist

"The Office will register works containing AI-generated material only if a human author contributed sufficient original expression. Sufficient human authorship exists when a human selects or arranges AI-generated content in a sufficiently creative way, or when the human modifies AI-generated content to the degree that the modifications meet the originality standard." U.S. Copyright Office, Copyright and Artificial Intelligence — Part 2: Copyrightability (2025)
"The standard of originality is low, but it does exist. … [T]he requisite level of creativity is extremely low; even a slight amount will suffice." Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Telephone Service Co., 499 U.S. 340, 345 (1991)

How Little Is Enough?

The 2025 USCO Report confirms that registration is available for works where a human has made even minimal creative choices over AI output. The Office has registered works where human authorship consisted solely of selecting which AI images to include in a collection. Feist at 345 sets the bar as "a slight amount" of creativity — not a substantial contribution, not a transformation, not an artistic statement.

In this demo, selecting the AI image (editorial judgment = authorial act) and applying any single edit (a brush stroke, a slider adjustment, a repositioning) jointly provide the minimal "selection and arrangement" or "modification" required. The additional caption, arrangement, and export bonuses are not required for protection.

What counts as human creative choice?

Selection — choosing to use an AI-generated image is editorial judgment, an authorial act recognised since Feist and reaffirmed by USCO 2025.

Transformation — applying any filter, making a brush mark, or repositioning an image layers human aesthetic judgment over the raw AI output, crossing the modification threshold.

Caption/Text — adding even a short caption creates independently authored literary expression, a separate copyrightable element.

Arrangement — how an image is positioned within a composition is itself protectable under 17 U.S.C. § 103(a) as compilation authorship.
