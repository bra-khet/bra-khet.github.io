FACT CHECK AI Energy Usage vs. Everyday Activities

A viral infographic claims that scrolling Instagram, watching Netflix, playing video games, and even video calls all consume more energy per hour than using AI. We verify each claim against 2024–2025 measurements. Click any row below to jump to the sourced breakdown.

Methodology Note

All estimates represent total system electricity per hour of activity — this includes the client device, network transmission, data-center computation, and cooling overhead. Values are derived from 2024–2025 peer-reviewed and industry measurements. Exact figures vary by device (smartphone vs. RTX 4090 gaming rig), video resolution, network type (Wi-Fi vs. cellular), and geographic grid carbon intensity. The AI baseline uses conversational sessions (~30–60 queries/hour) and excludes training cost, which is a one-time sunk cost not incurred per query. See each source card for full derivation details and citations.

01

Scrolling Instagram

✓ Confirmed
Infographic claim: 4–5× more energy than AI per hour
Actual estimate
0.05–0.10 kWh/hour (some heavy-feed measurements: 0.12–0.20 kWh)
AI baseline
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour → ratio: ~2.5–8×, with 4–5× as a solid midrange
Derivation
High-data image/video feed (~10–15 MB/min) + device screen and GPU + CDN and server load.
What does this mean?

Instagram's endless scroll burns roughly 4–5× as much electricity per hour as a typical AI chat session. The main reason is the sheer volume of images and short videos being delivered from global content networks — all of that data transfer and decoding adds up, even on a phone.

Sources

  • App-level energy audits (Greenspector 2020–2025 updates): consistent with 4–5× AI baseline
  • Device + data-center energy models: high-resolution feed usage aligns with these ranges

02

Watching Netflix / YouTube

✓ Confirmed
Infographic claim: 7× more energy than AI per hour
Actual estimate
0.077–0.12 kWh/hour (HD streaming)
AI baseline
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour → ratio: ~4–10×, placing 7× squarely in range
Derivation
Continuous video encoding/decoding at CDN scale dominates. Assumes standard HD bitrate on an average device.
What does this mean?

Streaming HD video is one of the most energy-intensive things you can do on the internet, hour for hour. The combination of high-bitrate delivery from distributed servers, your device decoding that stream in real time, and your screen running at full brightness all add up to roughly 7× the footprint of an AI conversation.

Sources

  • TRG Datacenters report (December 2025, cited in Forbes/ITWire): 0.12 kWh/hour — 42 g CO₂e at average grid intensity
  • IEA / Carbon Trust corrected analyses (2020/2023, still referenced 2025): ~0.077 kWh/hour for HD video streaming

03

Playing PC Video Games

✓ Confirmed
Infographic claim: 12–25× more energy than AI per hour
Actual estimate
0.20–0.50+ kWh/hour (mid-to-high-end gaming PC under load)
AI baseline
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour → ratio: ~10–40×, so 12–25× is a conservative midrange
Derivation
Gaming GPU draw: 200–500 W under load. Monitor: 50–100 W. A 300 W average system for one hour = 0.30 kWh, 15–25× the AI baseline.
What does this mean?

A gaming PC running a modern title at high settings draws as much power as several household appliances running simultaneously. The GPU alone — doing real-time physics, lighting, and rendering — consumes more electricity in an hour than an AI server handles in dozens of conversations. This is the biggest multiplier in the infographic, and it's actually understated for high-end setups.

Sources

  • Hardware power studies (EnergySage, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory analogs): 200–800 W load range, practical average 300–500 W during active gaming sessions

04

Video Calls (Zoom / Teams)

~ Roughly Accurate
Infographic claim: 6–7× more energy than AI per hour
Actual estimate
0.016–0.05 kWh/hour (per participant), most measurements cluster near 0.049 kWh
AI baseline
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour → ratio: ~2.5–4× using central estimates
Derivation
Video encoding/decoding + camera + bidirectional network transmission. Resolution-dependent — HD video calls use significantly more than SD.
What does this mean?

Video calls do use more energy than AI — but the 6–7× figure is on the high end of what the data supports. Central measurements put the actual ratio closer to 2.5–4×. The claim is directionally correct (video calls are more expensive than AI), but the magnitude depends heavily on video quality settings, your device, and whether you're using a phone or laptop. The infographic rounds up.

Sources

  • TRG Datacenters report (2025): 0.0486 kWh/hour per user — 17 g CO₂e at average grid intensity
  • Purdue University and independent measurements (2023–2025): range of 0.016–0.05 kWh per participant per hour

05

Music Streaming

~ Roughly Accurate
Infographic claim: 2–3× more energy than AI per hour
Actual estimate
0.02–0.05 kWh/hour (often closer to the lower end, sometimes below)
AI baseline
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour → ratio: ~1–4×, with 2–3× only at higher streaming usage
Derivation
Audio bitrates are ~50× less data-intensive than video. Device overhead (screen, cellular) often dominates over the actual streaming infrastructure cost.
What does this mean?

Music streaming is genuinely low-energy compared to video — some estimates put it at roughly 1 gram of CO₂ per hour, which is surprisingly close to AI. The 2–3× claim is plausible at the higher end of the measurement range, but many real-world measurements put them much closer to parity. On a phone with Wi-Fi, the gap between Spotify and a Claude conversation may be quite small.

Sources

  • Greenly / Carbon Trust (2024–2025): ~1 g CO₂e/hour — very low kWh equivalent, near AI baseline
  • Streaming service environmental reports (Spotify, Apple Music methodology disclosures)

06

Using AI for One Hour

✓ Confirmed
Infographic claim: Uses the least energy ✓
Actual estimate
0.012–0.020 kWh/hour (12–20 Wh) for a conversational session
Per-query cost
~0.24–0.34 Wh per text query on optimized modern models (H100/H200 GPUs)
Derivation
~30–60 short-to-medium queries per hour. Example: 40 queries × 0.30 Wh/query = 12 Wh. Training cost is excluded — it is a one-time sunk cost, not incurred per conversation.
What does this mean?

Modern AI inference is far more efficient than most people assume. A full hour of conversation with a large language model uses less electricity than leaving an LED bulb on. The efficiency has improved roughly 10× since early 2023 as hardware and model optimization improved. The "AI uses enormous energy" narrative applies far more accurately to the one-time cost of training — not to using the model.

Sources

  • Epoch AI (February 2025): 0.3 Wh median per GPT-4o query — a 10× improvement vs. early 2023 estimates
  • Google Cloud (August 2025): 0.24 Wh median per Gemini text query
  • OpenAI CEO statement (June 2025): ~0.34 Wh per typical query
