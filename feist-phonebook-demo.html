How the Feist Threshold Works

The Supreme Court held that facts are not copyrightable; only original creative expression is. Alphabetical arrangement of names is so obvious it lacks even a "modicum of creativity." But curating which subscribers to include and how to arrange them — with your own editorial commentary — can add enough originality to earn thin copyright protection.

Legal Foundation — Feist (1991)

"The sine qua non of copyright is originality. To qualify for copyright protection, a work must be original to the author… Original, as the term is used in copyright, means only that the work was independently created by the author (as opposed to copied from other works), and that it possesses at least some minimal degree of creativity." Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Tel. Serv. Co., 499 U.S. 340, 345 (1991)
"The selection, coordination, and arrangement of Rural's white pages do not satisfy the minimum constitutional standards for copyright protection… Rural's selection of listings — subscribers who pay for telephone service — is not original. Rural simply takes the data provided by its subscribers and lists it alphabetically by surname." Id. at 362–363

What does the demo measure?

Arrangement divergence (60%) — How many records have you moved from the default A→Z order? Feist says alphabetical sorting is so obvious it adds zero creativity. Any deliberate departure from the obvious arrangement signals authorial choice.

Commentary richness (40%) — Editorial notes, annotations, and descriptions added alongside each entry are independently authored expression. Even a brief curatorial remark can carry original expression that the underlying facts do not.

The 33% threshold

This demo uses 33% as the operational "modicum of creativity" marker — not a statutory figure, but calibrated to require at least moderate arrangement changes plus some commentary before flipping the status. In practice, courts apply a very low bar: some creative judgment suffices.
