Why A's facts earn 0 pts

Under Feist, facts are not original to the person who discovers or records them. Even if Contributor A spent months gathering and verifying data, each individual fact — and a purely mechanical arrangement of those facts — remains in the public domain. Legal Foundation — Joint Authorship

"A 'joint work' is a work prepared by two or more authors with the intention that their contributions be merged into inseparable or interdependent parts of a unitary whole." 17 U.S.C. § 101
"We hold that [for joint authorship] each contribution must be separately copyrightable… requiring each contribution to be independently copyrightable prevents a party who contributes only uncopyrightable material from claiming joint authorship of a work and gaining a share in the copyright." Erickson v. Trinity Theatre, Inc., 13 F.3d 1061, 1071 (7th Cir. 1994) — applying Feist to joint works

The independent copyrightability requirement

Courts applying the Seventh and Ninth Circuit standard require that each joint author's contribution be independently copyrightable — it must meet Feist's originality standard on its own. Contributor A's bare facts, however diligently gathered, cannot satisfy this. Contributor B's aesthetic and narrative choices can — and when combined with A's substantive content, they create a joint work in which B holds copyright in the creative elements.

Moral of the demo

Even in collaborative works, the Feist standard acts as a gatekeeper at the individual-contribution level. A "joint" work does not allow one party to launder uncopyrightable material into protection by partnering with a creative author — each author's own contribution must cross the threshold independently.
